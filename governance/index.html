1. Purpose

This document explains how equity vesting works at Stackable. It's intended for board members, key hires, advisers, and investors. The terms here are already reflected in our shareholders' agreement and option grant documentation.

2. Why this policy exists

When someone joins a company early, they're often given ownership, which is a percentage of the company, expressed as shares. This is how startups attract good people when they can't yet compete on salary.

The problem is timing. Ownership given upfront, all at once, creates a perverse situation: someone can receive their full stake in the company, then leave the next month, and still own it. They've contributed almost nothing, but they hold equity as if they built the place. The people who stay are left building value for someone who already walked away.

Vesting solves this by making ownership something you earn over time rather than receive all at once. Leave after six months, and you own very little. Stay for four years, and you've earned the lot. The equity tracks the work.

This policy applies to everyone at Stackable (including founder(s), key hires, board members).

3. Two types of equity at Stackable

There are two ways people receive equity here. Founders receive shares directly. Key hires and advisers receive options (i.e., the right to buy shares at a fixed price in the future). Both vest on the same schedule. But the legal structure, mechanics, and tax treatment are different.

Founder Shares Key Hire / Advisor Options
Instrument Ordinary shares issued upfront. Options (right to buy shares at a set price).
Vesting schedule 4 years, 1-year cliff 4 years, 1-year cliff
Legal structure Reverse vesting. Company holds repurchase right. ESOP. Options granted, exercised after vesting.

4. Founder Equity

4.1 The Vesting Schedule

The shares don't arrive all at once. They accumulate over four years.

The first twelve months is a probationary period of sorts, nothing is earned during this time. It exists because both sides need to find out if this is actually going to work. If it isn't, a clean separation is easier when no equity has changed hands yet. This period is called the cliff.

At the twelve-month mark, a quarter of the total shares vest at once. After that, the remaining shares arrive in small amounts each month over the following four years. By year four, the schedule is complete.

Milestone Vesting Event Cumulative Vested
Month 0–11 Nothing vests (cliff period) 0%
Month 12 25% vests in one tranche 25%
Months 13–48 1/48th vests each month 25% → 100%
Month 48 Final tranche vests 100%

4.2 The Australian Structure: Reverse Vesting

Founder shares are issued upfront, with the company holding a contractual right to repurchase the unvested portion. As milestones are achieved, this right expires for each tranche. If a founder departs before the schedule ends, the company repurchases the unvested shares at their original issue price. This process, known as reverse vesting, is common in Australia and achieves similar results to the US model through a different legal framework.

This repurchase right is outlined in the shareholders' agreement. Board members should ensure that this agreement is current and updated after each investment round.

4.3 Credit for Work Before the Investment

If the founder has been working on the company for a meaningful period before the raise, up to 25% of their shares may be exempt from the vesting schedule. This recognises that the clock didn't start on the day the cheque arrived. Any such credit is disclosed to all shareholders and documented in the shareholders' agreement. The board should review and sign off on this before it is agreed.

5. Key Hire & Advisor Options

5.1 Understanding what an option is

When a startup is young, it has very little cash but — if things go well — significant future value. Options are a way of sharing that future value with the people who help create it.

You are not given any shares when you join. Instead you are given a right — the right to purchase shares at whatever they are worth on the day you joined. That price is locked in from day one.

If you stay, and the company grows, you can exercise that right at a future point — typically any time within ten years. When you do, you buy shares at the old join-day price. What you actually receive is the difference between that price and what the shares are worth now. If shares were worth $1 when you joined and are worth $20 when you exercise, you pay $1 and receive something worth $20. That $19 gap is yours.

If the company doesn't grow — if shares are still worth $1, or worth less — you simply don't buy. The option expires unused. You've lost nothing except the time you invested.

This is what makes options work as an incentive. They have no value unless the company becomes more valuable. Which means the person holding them has a direct personal interest in making that happen.

5.2 Vesting Schedule

4 years, 1-year cliff. Nothing vests in the first 12 months. At month 12, 25% vests. The rest comes through monthly over the following three years. Options that are not yet vested lapse on departure.

5.3 Exercise and Expiry

Vested options don't last forever. Key terms:

  • Options expire 10 years from the date of grant if not exercised.
  • Good leavers typically have up to 12 months after departure to exercise vested options before they lapse.
  • Bad leavers typically have 90 days. After that, unexercised options lapse regardless of vesting status.
  • On acquisition or IPO, vested options are typically exercised as part of the transaction. The board should ensure option agreements address this explicitly.

5.4 Employee Share Scheme Rules

Employee Share Scheme (ESS) rules under Division 83A of the ITAA 1997 apply to options granted to employees and advisers.

The good news: most early-stage Australian startups qualify for the startup concession, which makes the tax treatment much more favourable:

  • No tax when options are granted.
  • No tax when options vest.
  • No tax when options are exercised (this is the big one, in the standard ESS regime, there can be tax at exercise even before you've sold any shares).
  • Tax only applies when you sell the resulting shares. If held for more than 12 months from the date of exercise, the 50% CGT discount applies.

To qualify for the startup concession, the company must be unlisted, less than 10 years old, have annual turnover under $50 million, and the options must be granted at or above fair market value. Stackable Group currently meets these criteria. This should be confirmed at each grant date.

Independent tax advice is recommended for all option holders. This document is not tax advice.

6. What happens when someone leaves

The leaver provisions apply to both founders and option holders, though the mechanics differ slightly. For the board: classifying a departure as good leaver or bad leaver is a governance decision with financial consequences.

Category Trigger Events Founder Shares Options
Good Leaver Serious illness, permanent injury, death, or other agreed exceptional circumstances Retains vested shares. Unvested bought back at issue price. Up to 12 months to exercise vested options. Unvested lapse.
Bad Leaver Fraud, criminal offence, material breach of duties, or voluntary resignation without cause Vested shares repurchased at up to 50% discount to FMV (Square Peg standard — often negotiated lower). Unvested bought back at issue price. 90 days to exercise vested options. Unvested lapse. Unexercised options lapse after 90 days.
Exit / Acquisition Trade sale, merger, acquisition, or other liquidity event All unvested shares accelerate and vest immediately. All unvested options accelerate. Typically exercised as part of the transaction.

All founder and key-hire agreements include non-compete and non-solicitation clauses that apply for a reasonable period after departure. The board should satisfy itself that these are appropriately scoped and enforceable under Australian law.

8. What the Board needs to own

Four things the board should have visibility on and take responsibility for.

Documentation.
The shareholders' agreement and option plan documentation need to be current, correctly drafted, and updated after each investment round. If you're joining the board and haven't reviewed these, please ask.

Leaver decisions.
When a founder or key hire leaves, the board will be involved in classifying the departure and deciding whether to exercise repurchase rights. The better the definitions are in the underlying documents, the cleaner that process will be.

Option grants.
Every new option grant needs board approval. That includes the number of options, the exercise price (which must reflect fair market value for the startup concession to apply), and the vesting terms. Getting it wrong has tax consequences for the recipient.

ESS compliance.
If the company no longer qualifies for the startup concession, because it's been listed, exceeded the age or turnover thresholds, or options are being granted below fair market value, the board needs to know, because the tax consequences for option holders change materially.

Your Company Constitution in Plain English

What the AIC Open Source Constitution actually means for you as a founder

A constitution is the rulebook for how your company operates. It replaces the default "replaceable rules" in the Corporations Act. You need one because the defaults can't handle investor shares, and investors like Blackbird won't invest without one. This is the industry-standard template that Blackbird co-created. Here's what each part actually does.

1 Share classes

Your company can issue different types of shares. Ordinary shares are yours as founder — they give you voting rights, dividends, and a share of proceeds if the company is sold. Seed Preference Shares are what investors get. They come with extra protections (detailed in Schedule 1). Directors can create and issue shares without a full shareholder vote each time.

2 Investor protections (Schedule 1)

Investor shares convert into ordinary shares (initially 1:1). They vote alongside you on an as-converted basis — so 1 preference share = 1 vote. They get dividends equal to what they'd receive if they'd already converted. The key protections are the liquidation preference and anti-dilution (see boxes 3 and 4).

3 Liquidation preference

If the company is sold, wound up, or distributes its assets, investors get paid first. They receive the greater of: (a) their original investment back, or (b) what they'd get if they converted to ordinary shares. This is called 1x non-participating — they pick the better option but can't take both. This is the founder-friendliest standard.

4 Anti-dilution

If you later raise money at a lower price than what investors paid (a "down round"), their conversion ratio adjusts so they get more ordinary shares on conversion. It uses a broad-based weighted average formula — the industry standard. It does not apply to employee option grants, bonus issues, or acquisition-related share issues.

5 Share transfers & restrictions

You can't freely sell your shares. Directors can refuse to register any transfer. If there's a Shareholders Deed (there will be), its transfer rules override the constitution. This keeps control over who becomes a shareholder — critical for a small company with investors on the cap table.

6 Directors & decision-making

Directors manage the company's business. The board can appoint new directors, delegate to committees, and borrow money. Directors must disclose personal interests in transactions. Meetings can be held virtually or in person. The chair does not get a casting vote — deadlocks must be resolved, not overruled.

7 Shareholders Deed trumps

Where the Shareholders Deed and constitution conflict, the Shareholders Deed wins — except for the preference share terms in Schedule 1, which override everything. The Shareholders Deed is a separate document covering things like drag-along, tag-along, founder vesting, board seats, and reserved matters.

8 Dividends, indemnity & winding up

Directors decide if and when to pay dividends. On winding up, the liquidator can distribute assets in kind (not just cash). Officers (including you as director) are indemnified against liability incurred in their role, and the company can pay for D&O insurance — as long as there's no wilful breach of duty.

What this constitution does NOT cover — and what you'll need separately: founder vesting schedules, drag-along and tag-along rights, board composition and investor director seats, reserved matters requiring investor approval, ESOP/option pool terms, and non-compete obligations. These all live in the Shareholders Deed, which you'll negotiate alongside this constitution when you raise.
