The most misused tool
in every school.

Tom eats lunch at the same spot every day. Same desk. Same chair. Same place next to the window where he lines up his sandwich, his apple, and his water bottle in the same order. One morning, the teacher rearranges the classroom for a group activity. The desks are pushed together in clusters. Tom walks in after the bell, sees his spot is gone, and stops in the doorway. His teacher aide tries to guide him to a new seat. He drops to the floor.

Key takeaway

Change can be very hard for kids like Tom. For example, if Tom comes to class and finds his desk has moved, he might not know what to do. He could get upset and sit on the floor.

This happens because Tom wasn't told about the change. When things are different from what he expects, it can be confusing and scary for him. If Tom is given a warning about changes, he can feel more comfortable and know what to do next time.

However, the issue may not be Tom's behaviour, but rather that he was not informed about the upcoming change.

What most people get wrong about Social Stories

Treating them as behaviour management. Social Stories are not about getting a child to stop doing something. They are about helping a child understand something. The moment you write a story to fix a behaviour the adults find annoying, you have missed the point entirely.

Setting goals that are too vague. "Help Tom with social skills" is not a goal. It is a wish. A Social Story needs one clear, specific moment to focus on.

Reading the story once or twice and expecting results. One reading does not stick. Two readings do not stick. Children who heard the story at least six times did noticeably better than those who heard it fewer times.

Writing the story for the adult, not the child. If Tom hums during quiet reading and it bothers the teacher but Tom is perfectly happy, a Social Story is not the answer. The child is not struggling. The adult is.

Reading it at the wrong time. Five minutes before lunch is the worst possible moment. Tom is already bracing. A calm, settled moment earlier in the day is where the story needs to happen.

Overselling it to parents. Social Stories help with one thing at a time. When you tell a parent it will improve their child's social skills overall, you are setting everyone up for disappointment.

What you need to know

They are a torch, not a floodlight. A story about "being a good friend" lights up nothing. A story about "what to do when someone sits in your usual seat" lights up exactly the moment Tom needs help with. He can picture it. He can prepare for it. The narrower the focus, the better it works.

The goal-setting conversation is worth as much as the story. When the teacher, parent, and child sit down together and move past "Tom struggles socially" to "Tom doesn't know what to do when the class moves from the carpet to their desks, and he freezes every single time," the adults align. They stop guessing. They agree on what Tom actually needs.

Repetition is not optional. Autistic children take in and remember information by hearing it repeatedly. A story read once will not be there when the real moment arrives. Tom needs to hear it enough times that the information is already in place when he walks into the classroom, and the desks have moved.
