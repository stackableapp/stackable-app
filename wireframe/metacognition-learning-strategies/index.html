Metacognition & Learning Strategies
Screen builder note
Metacognition in e-learning = giving learners the tools to manage their own thinking. Three distinct components shown: (1) Pre-lesson Planning Prompt — learner enters intentionally rather than clicking next; (2) Strategy Matcher — matches study strategy to learning goal; (3) Concrete Strategy Library — 8 specific techniques with short how-to descriptions. Each component is embeddable independently.

3
Concrete Strategy Library
8 specific techniques — each with a short how-to. Browse and select. Builds repertoire over time.
R
Retrieval practice
Close notes. Try to recall from memory. Write it. Check. Repeat on gaps. Stronger than rereading.
Q
Self-questioning
After each section, ask: what was the main point? Why does this matter? What would happen if this were different?
W
Worked example first
Study the worked example carefully. Explain each step to yourself. Then try a similar problem independently.
C
Compare and contrast
Take two similar concepts. Write down what they share and how they differ. Forces you to understand both.
S
Summarise in your own words
After reading, close the material and write a 2–3 sentence summary from memory in plain language.
✓
Checklist before submitting
Before you submit, check your work against the criteria one criterion at a time. Do not skim — verify each.
B
Break into steps
Write out every step needed before you start. Work through them one at a time. Do not skip ahead.
M
Monitor confidence vs performance
Rate your confidence before attempting a task. Then check your actual result. Notice the gap — it reveals blind spots.
R
Expanded — when learner taps a strategy
Retrieval practice
Close your notes and try to recall what you just learned from memory. Write it out without looking. Then open your notes and check. Repeat specifically on the points you could not recall. The effort of retrieval — even when you fail — strengthens the memory far more than rereading the material.
